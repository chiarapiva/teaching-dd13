Hate: between ideology and violence

Hate in United States is hidden behind different faces.
Hate crimes seems to be falling, but there is an explosive rising of the hate groups.

HATE GROUPS

- the SPLC acknowledges alleged "hate group" activities include constitutionally protected activities such as "marches, rallies,speeches, meetings, leafleting or publishing"

The Southern Poverty Law Center defines hate groups as those that "... have beliefs or practices that attack or malign an entire class of people, typically for their immutable characteristics." The SPLC states that: "Hate group activities can include criminal acts, marches, rallies, speeches, meetings, leafleting or publishing." The SPLC adds that "Listing here does not imply that a group advocates or engages in violence or other criminal activity".

ucr.fbi.gov/hate-crime

HATE CRIMES

- According to the FBI: "Hate itself is not a crime—and the FBI is mindful of protecting freedom of speech and other civil liberties."

The Federal Bureau of Investigation defines a hate crime as a "criminal offense against a person, property, or society which is motivated, in whole or in part, by the offender’s bias against a race, religion, disability, sexual orientation, or ethnicity/national origin." The vast majority of hate crimes are cases of assault or vandalism.The critical identifying element of hate crimes is the bias motivation of the perpetrator.

splcenter.org/hate-map

The shared source of Hate

In order to classify and define two different datasets we identified four main categories: Race, Religion, LGBT and Other. In the alluvial diagram we can observe the connection of the two different forms of hate (hate crimes and hate groups) with the categories to which they belong. It is immediately evident that race is the predominant one. Moreover, the diagram displays that the category of race is more prevailing in hate groups than in hate crimes.



A new shape of United States

In the map below, the dimension of each state is given by the amount of hate crimes of every state, while the intensity of the colour represents the numerical value of the hate crimes per capita. On the left we can see the total amount of hate groups in United States and selecting a specific state on the map is possible to visualize the groups state by state.

US Hate: a closer view on its present and past

Opposite paths through the decade

The following flows describe the evolution of hate groups and hate crimes through years, from 2000 up to today. By comparison, the different trends highlight a general rise of hate groups on one hand (particularly massive for race category) and an opposite decrease of hate crimes on the other. These opposite trends underline that hate is changing its shape: it persists much more in ideological forms that in physical ones.

Moreover, these data put a new question about where these ideological manifestations of hate take place today. Finally, building on that, the research is going to turn toward the analysis of other channels of the hate diffusion, instead of the physical manifestation through crimes. For instance, is interesting to explore how the online presence of hate groups has risen in 2016 and how hate speeches are changing in the latest years.
